Here‚Äôs what happened, and some tips on how you can protect yourself from a similar fate. Earlier this month, KrebsOnSecurity heard from Paul Rosenzweig, a 27-year-old T-Mobile customer from Boston who had his wireless account briefly hijacked. Rosenzweig had previously adopted T-Mobile‚Äôs advice to customers about blocking mobile number port-out scams, an increasingly common scheme in which identity thieves armed with a fake ID in the name of a targeted customer show up at a retail store run by a different wireless provider and ask that the number to be transferred to the competing mobile company‚Äôs network. So-called ‚Äúport out‚Äù scams allow crooks to intercept your calls and messages while your phone goes dark. Porting a number to a new provider shuts off the phone of the original user, and forwards all calls to the new device. Once in control of the mobile number, thieves who have already stolen a target‚Äôs password(s) can request any second factor that is sent to the newly activated device, such as a one-time code sent via text message or or an automated call that reads the one-time code aloud. In this case, however, the perpetrator didn‚Äôt try to port Rosenzweig‚Äôs phone number: Instead, the attacker called multiple T-Mobile retail stores within an hour‚Äôs drive of Rosenzweig‚Äôs home address until he succeeded in convincing a store employee to conduct what‚Äôs known as a ‚ÄúSIM swap.‚Äù A SIM swap is a legitimate process by which a customer can request that a new SIM card (the tiny, removable chip in a mobile device that allows it to connect to the provider‚Äôs network) be added to the account. Customers can request a SIM swap when their existing SIM card has been damaged, or when they are switching to a different phone that requires a SIM card of another size. However, thieves and other ne‚Äôer-do-wells can abuse this process by posing as a targeted mobile customer or technician and tricking employees at the mobile provider into swapping in a new SIM card for that customer on a device that they control. If successful, the SIM swap accomplishes more or less the same result as a number port out (at least in the short term) ‚Äî effectively giving the attackers access to any text messages or phone calls that are sent to the target‚Äôs mobile account. Rosenzweig said the first inkling he had that something wasn‚Äôt right with his phone was on the evening of May 2, 2018, when he spotted an automated email from Instagram. The message said the email address tied to the three-letter account he‚Äôd had on the social media platform for seven years ‚Äî instagram.com/par ‚Äî had been changed. He quickly logged in to his Instagram account, changed his password and then reverted the email on the account back to his original address. By this time, the SIM swap conducted by the attacker had already been carried out, although Rosenzweig said he didn‚Äôt notice his phone displaying zero bars and no connection to T-Mobile at the time because he was at home and happily surfing the Web on his device using his own wireless network. The following morning, Rosenzweig received another notice ‚Äî this one from Snapchat ‚Äî stating that the password for his account there (‚Äúp9r‚Äù) had been changed. Please note that a SIM lock on your mobile account is separate from a SIM PIN that you can set via your mobile phone‚Äôs operating system. A SIM PIN is essentially an additional layer of physical security that locks the current SIM to your device, requiring you to input a special PIN when the device is powered on in order to call, text or access your data plan on your phone. This feature can help block thieves from using your phone or accessing your data if you lose your phone, but it won‚Äôt stop thieves from physically swapping in their own SIM card. iPhone users can follow these instructions to set or change a device‚Äôs SIM PIN.
